Mental models

Learn about this concept and some of the best known ones.

Alejandro Moreno

Mental models are applicable schemes that help us to interpret the environment, providing results. There are different mental models applicable to different situations.

Users often take some of these mental models when interacting with our products. Knowing these models will help you to empathize with the user and to design in a more user-friendly way.

Here are some of these models and their application in product design.

Do what is important and ignore what is urgent

What is important is what you can't stop doing, even if the benefit of doing it or the delivery date is not a short deadline. The urgent, on the other hand, is that which demands an immediate response and usually comes from the environment.

Tip

Focus on the tasks or features that really drive the value of the product. This will help you stay on track and avoid dispersion.

Make reversible decisions

Keep a balance between reversible and irreversible decisions. Making reversible decisions makes you move forward faster and you can get a lot of information. However, we must make irreversible decisions so that we don't get stuck somewhere along the way.

Tip

When designing features or interfaces, consider reversible decisions. For instance, choose a design approach that allows tweaking or rolling back changes without major repercussions. This approach fosters experimentation and learning from mistakes.

Submit your perspective to an external evaluation

In order to solve problems more efficiently, we must subject our thinking to an outside perspective. Sometimes, we get caught up in thinking that keeps us circling back to the same idea and we can lose perspective on the real problem or focus energy on something that is irrelevant.

Tip

Regularly involve users, stakeholders, or usability experts in evaluating your designs. Conduct usability testing, gather feedback, and iterate based on external insights. An external perspective ensures a more holistic and user-centered approach.

Murphy's Law

If something can go wrong, it will. It is not about being pessimistic, but about putting solutions to every situation, so that there is no room for error. This perspective is very helpful, as long as you can visualize the situations that will arise as a consequence of your decisions.

Tip

Anticipate potential failures or issues. Design robust systems that gracefully handle errors or unexpected scenarios. Consider fallback mechanisms, error messages, and fail-safe features. Murphy's Law reminds us to be prepared for the worst.

The Pareto Principle

According to this principle, 20% of your actions produce 80% of your results. It is therefore about acquiring the ability to identify which are the 20% of actions that you must perform in order to achieve the results you set out to achieve.

Tip

Identify the vital few features or aspects that drive most value for users. Focus your efforts on these critical elements. Prioritize tasks, features, or improvements based on their impact. Pareto's principle guides efficient resource allocation and decision-making.
